🎯 Soonest Cold Call Guide

Quick reference for live calls

📟 Step 1 — Reaching the Gatekeeper (Receptionist)

What to say
"Hi there — I'm looking to speak with whoever oversees or builds out the staff and physician schedule. Is that person available?"
"Hi — who at your organization would be the right person to talk to about your staff scheduling process?"
Pro tip: If you already have a name (from research), use it: "Hi, I'm looking to speak with Michael — is he available?" Named asks skip the interrogation and feel more natural.

📞 Step 2 — Connected to the Decision-Maker

Opening pitch (tight version)
"Hi [name], Kade calling from Soonest — we build scheduling software specifically for emergency physician groups and urgent cares. Quick question — are you the person who handles the staff schedule for the group?"

(If yes, continue. If no, ask who is.)

Value pitch after confirming right person
"We help groups like yours cut the admin time on scheduling in half while giving your providers more of a say in their own schedules. I'd love to show you what that looks like — do you have 20 minutes this week or next?"
Alternative — insight-led opener
"We talk to a lot of EM groups and the thing we hear most isn't that their scheduling software is broken — it's that it's quietly costing them more than they realize in overtime, admin time, and staff frustration. I'd love 20 minutes to show you what we've been able to do for groups just like yours."

Free Schedule Offer

When to use it: Best as a closer when they're lukewarm — not as your opening ask. If they're engaged, push for the meeting first. Use the free schedule as your "yes ladder" when they hesitate.
"Here's what I'd suggest — we'll actually build out a free sample schedule for your group so your team can see exactly how Soonest would work for your operation before committing to anything. No strings attached. Worth a look?"
⚠️ Leading with "we'll build you a free schedule" before establishing any need can feel premature. It works better as a response to hesitation: "I get it — before we ask you to take time for a full demo, here's what we can do first..."

📬 Voicemail Script

"Hi [name], this is Kade Jensen calling from Soonest — 385-832-7669. We help emergency physician groups save hours of admin time on scheduling while giving their providers more control over their shifts. I'll shoot you an email too, but if you want to connect, I'm at 385-832-7669. Talk soon."
Keep voicemails under 25 seconds. Name, company, one-line value prop, number. Always follow with an email.

💡 Coaching Notes on Your Current Opener

  • Remove "like I mentioned" — it sounds like you're repeating yourself and signals filler
  • "Tomorrow or the next day" can feel pushy — try "this week or next" for less pressure
  • Confirm they're the right person before pitching — don't pitch the receptionist
  • Tailor "urgent care" vs "emergency group" based on who you're calling — don't say both
  • The free schedule is a strong offer but burns faster as an opener than as a closer

🚧 Common Gatekeeper Objections

"No worries at all — do you know when they might wrap up? I can call back at a better time."
"That's fine — what's usually a better window for them, morning or afternoon?"
Goal: Get a specific callback time. Avoid leaving a message — it gives them time to prepare a "no."
"Got it — when are they typically in? I want to make sure I catch them directly."
"Is there a direct line or extension I can use when I call back?"
Bonus: Ask for a direct number or extension. Even if they don't give it, it signals you're serious and often loosens up the gatekeeper.
"I appreciate that — honestly I'd rather just call back when they're free so I don't miss them. When's a good window?"
If they insist on taking a message: Leave your name and number, but say "I'll plan to follow up as well just in case — what's the best time?" Don't rely on callbacks.
⚠️ Messages rarely get returned. Treat it as intel-gathering (you confirmed there IS someone to reach) and call back on your own schedule.
"Of course — I'm reaching out about their staff scheduling process. It'll be a quick conversation."
"Sure — we work with physician groups on scheduling. I just want two minutes with [name] to see if it's relevant for them."
Keep it vague enough to pass through, specific enough to sound legitimate. Never say "I'm trying to sell them something."
"Totally understand — I'm not trying to pitch anything today, I just had a quick question for [name] about how your group currently handles scheduling. Would it be okay if I called back at a better time?"
Disarming the word "solicitation" with "I just had a question" often gets you back in. The goal is just to get to the right person — you can pitch them yourself.

🔑 Gatekeeper Strategy Tips

  • Always research the decision-maker's name before calling — named asks have higher pass-through rates
  • Be warm and brief — receptionists respond to people who aren't annoyed or rushed
  • If you've been blocked 2–3 times, try calling at a different time of day — a different person may answer
  • Try the direct number from ZoomInfo or LinkedIn if available — skips the front desk entirely
  • If you get a name from the receptionist, use it on your next call: "Hi, is [name] available?"
  • Email + call combo: send a short email first, then call and reference it to get past "take a message"

🚨 Last-Minute Callouts

Key insight: Nearly half of physicians already work with understaffed teams more than 25% of the time — those physicians are 2x more likely to burn out. Callouts accelerate this cycle.
  • Callouts trigger 1.5x overtime pay + increased payroll taxes + higher workers' comp premiums
  • Over-reliance on core staff causes morale drops → more callouts → harder to retain staff
  • 34% of healthcare workers have on-call arrangements — highest of any industry

Questions to ask

  • "When someone calls out last minute, what does that process actually look like for whoever manages it?"
  • "How long does it typically take to get that shift covered?"
  • "Is there a go-to person who gets called, or does it fall on whoever's available?"
  • "How often would you say that happens in a given month?"

QGenda / Shift Admin — Overview

  • Rules-based scheduling engine — requires admins to build and maintain all rules manually
  • Shift Admin acquired by QGenda in 2019 — essentially the same platform now
  • Used widely by large hospital systems and physician groups
  • Customer support was outsourced ~2023 — major source of current frustration
  • Expensive — barrier for mid-size groups

⚡ Key Weaknesses to Probe

Pain Point
  • Automation breaks on complex rules — users go manual
  • No dedicated support rep anymore
  • Mobile app login is a daily frustration for providers
  • Setup requires IT-level maintenance ongoing
  • Not built for provider experience — admin tool only
Questions to Surface It
  • "Is it actually building the schedule automatically, or do you adjust it manually?"
  • "Do you have a dedicated rep, or is it the general support queue?"
  • "What do your providers say about using the mobile app?"
  • "When something changes in your operation, how easy is it to update QGenda?"

💬 How to Position Soonest Against QGenda

"QGenda requires someone to build and maintain the rules. When your operation changes, someone has to update the system. With Soonest, the AI adapts — you're not the one keeping it current."
"You've already proven you'll invest in scheduling software. The question is whether what you have is keeping pace with what's possible — or just keeping pace with what was possible five years ago."
"Most groups using QGenda are still doing more manual work than they realize. The software handles the calendar. The admin is still handling the thinking."

When I Work — Overview

General shift scheduling — NOT built for healthcare
  • Designed primarily for retail, restaurants, and hourly workers
  • Basic drag-and-drop scheduling with simple shift management
  • No physician-specific features — no call scheduling, no equitable distribution algorithms
  • No credential or compliance tracking
  • Low price point — appealing to smaller urgent cares watching budget
  • Simple enough that many staff actually like the interface

🔴 Key Weaknesses for Medical Groups

  • No intelligent shift distribution — admin manually ensures fairness
  • Can't handle complex on-call, coverage requirements, or specialty assignments
  • No built-in way to manage preference requests at scale
  • Swap process is basic — no approval routing or conflict checking
  • Reporting is limited — hard to track equity or hours across providers
  • Not designed for 24/7 emergency department complexity
⚠️ Groups using When I Work are likely a smaller urgent care — they may have outgrown it or don't know what better looks like. This is a great free-schedule-offer target.

⚡ Questions to Ask When I Work Users

  • "How do you make sure shifts are distributed fairly across your providers?"
  • "When a provider wants to swap a shift, what does that process look like?"
  • "Do you ever run into situations where someone gets more undesirable shifts than others?"
  • "How do you track who's worked how many hours across different locations or shift types?"

💬 How to Position Against When I Work

"When I Work is great for restaurants and retail — it wasn't built for the complexity of a physician group. The difference is pretty stark once you see it."
"A lot of groups start on something like When I Work because it's simple, but as you grow or add providers, you hit a ceiling pretty fast."

Intrigma — Overview

  • Healthcare-specific scheduling platform built for physician groups
  • Has AI-assisted scheduling capabilities — closer to Soonest's positioning
  • Better physician-facing experience than QGenda but still admin-heavy setup
  • Smaller company — support can be inconsistent
  • Often chosen by groups who found QGenda too complex

🔴 Key Weaknesses

  • Less robust mobile experience — providers still rely on desktop
  • Implementation can take weeks to months
  • Limited integration with payroll and HR systems
  • Customer support is smaller team — slower response times
  • Less intuitive for new admins — learning curve similar to QGenda

⚡ Questions to Ask Intrigma Users

  • "How long did it take you to get fully set up and running on Intrigma?"
  • "Are your providers using the mobile app, or do they mostly check the schedule another way?"
  • "When you need support, how responsive is the team?"
  • "Are there things you wish it could do that it can't?"

💬 How to Position Against Intrigma

"Intrigma moved in the right direction — they're healthcare-specific. Where Soonest is different is the staff experience side. We're designed for what the providers actually see and interact with, not just what the admin sees."

Payroll Systems (ADP, Paycor, Paylocity, Paychex) — Overview

Built for payroll — scheduling is an afterthought
  • ADP, Paycor, Paylocity, and Paychex are payroll and HR platforms — scheduling is a bolt-on feature, not their core product
  • Work fine for simple, static schedules (same shifts, same people, week over week)
  • Common in urgent cares that haven't outgrown basic scheduling needs yet
  • Admins often say it's "easy" or "takes 5 minutes" — usually means the schedule never changes
  • Staff experience is poor — employees have to log into a payroll portal to see their schedule
  • No intelligent shift distribution, no preference engine, no AI — just a calendar grid
⚠️ The "5 minutes a month" admin is usually running the same schedule on repeat. The real question is what happens when something changes.

⚡ Where Payroll Systems Break Down for Medical Groups

The Hidden Pain Points
  • Last-minute callouts — no automated coverage workflow; someone manually calls or texts staff
  • Swaps — providers can't self-service swap shifts; admin has to manually update
  • Preferences — no way to capture or honor provider schedule preferences at scale
  • Fair distribution — no equity engine; admin manually ensures no one is overloaded
  • Multi-location — works until you have float staff or variable hours; then it breaks
  • Staff experience — employees log into a payroll portal to check shifts — not a scheduling app
  • Growth — "5 minutes" works for 10 staff on a static schedule; breaks fast when you scale
The "5 Minutes" Problem
When an admin says it takes 5 minutes/month, it almost always means:
  • The same schedule repeats every week
  • Staff rarely request changes
  • Callouts are handled informally (texts/calls)
  • They've never seen what a purpose-built scheduling tool looks like

The 5 minutes is just building the template. The hours are in everything around it.

⚡ Questions to Surface the Hidden Pain

  • "When someone calls out last minute, what does that process look like — how do you find coverage?"
  • "If a staff member wants to swap a shift or request a specific day off, how do they do that?"
  • "Does your staff check their schedule through Paycor/ADP, or do they find out another way?"
  • "How do you make sure the same people aren't always stuck with the less desirable shifts?"
  • "What happens when you have a new hire or someone changes their availability — how long does that take to update?"
  • "If I asked your staff how easy it is to see and manage their schedule, what do you think they'd say?"
  • "Are you planning to add any locations or staff in the next year or two?" (growth = complexity = need)

💬 How to Position Soonest Against Payroll Systems

"Paycor can do scheduling the same way a spreadsheet can — technically yes, but it's not built for it. The moment something changes or your staff wants more visibility, you're doing everything manually."
"The scheduling part of those platforms is designed to be good enough — not great. What we hear most from groups that switch is that the 5 minutes of building the schedule wasn't the problem. It was everything around it — the callouts, the swaps, the staff complaints."
"I totally get that it feels easy right now. What we usually find is the time isn't in building the schedule — it's in managing everything the schedule creates. Last-minute gaps, shift changes, staff asking questions. That's what we eliminate."
Best play with "5 minutes" groups: Don't argue with them on admin time. Pivot to the staff experience and callout coverage angles — those are where the real pain lives and where they're most likely to feel it.
